Solving any triangle from three values

July 8, 2026·Stephan·±4 min read

The Triangle Calculator's official tagline is "I many smart now!", which undersells it slightly: give it any three independent values of a triangle — sides or angles — and it hands back everything else: the missing sides, the missing angles, the area, the perimeter. Here's the complete bag of tricks it uses, which is also the entire trigonometry you need for woodworking, roofing, or helping with homework you claimed to remember.

Which three values are enough?

A triangle has six quantities: three sides (a, b, c) and three angles (A, B, C). Knowing three of them pins down the triangle — with two exceptions. Three angles (AAA) only fix the shape, not the size: every scale of that triangle has the same angles. And one particular combination gives two valid answers (see below).

The four solvable cases

Law of cosines

c² = a² + b² − 2ab·cos(C)

Pythagoras with a correction term. When C = 90°, cos(C) = 0 and it collapses into the a² + b² = c² you already know.

Law of sines

a / sin(A) = b / sin(B) = c / sin(C)

Every side divided by the sine of its opposite angle gives the same ratio (the diameter of the circumscribed circle, if you want to impress someone).

The ambiguous case: when two answers are both right

SSA — two sides and an angle that isn't between them — is the famous trap. Example: a = 7, b = 10, A = 40°. The law of sines gives sin(B) ≈ 0.918… but two angles have that sine: B ≈ 66.7° and B ≈ 113.3°. Both produce a perfectly valid triangle. A good calculator shows both solutions instead of silently picking one; a bad one loses you a roof truss.

Area without knowing the height

Two favourites:

Area = ½ · a · b · sin(C)          (two sides + included angle)

s = (a + b + c) / 2                (Heron's formula, three sides)
Area = √( s(s−a)(s−b)(s−c) )

Heron's formula is 2,000 years old and still the best party trick in geometry: area from nothing but the three side lengths.
